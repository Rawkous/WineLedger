From grape to glass

A concise, classroom-friendly path through real-world wine production — the same stages your WineLedger simulation encodes as blocks. Use it alongside the canvas to connect process, data, and transparency.

How this pairs with WineLedger

Each time you run /simulate-once, the app appends six events: HARVEST → FERMENTATION → BARREL_AGING → BOTTLING → TRANSPORT → RETAIL. On the ledger they are immutable records; on the canvas they become color and motion. This page explains why those stages exist in the real industry, so learners can interpret the visuals as more than abstract art.

Ledger: HARVEST

Harvest

Grapes are picked when sugar, acid, and flavor compounds reach a target profile — by hand in steep vineyards or by machine on flatter ground. Timing shifts with weather; heat waves or rain can move the picking window by days. In teaching, harvest is a natural entry point for sensor data (Brix, pH) and provenance (block, clone, crew).

Ledger: FERMENTATION

Fermentation

Yeasts (often Saccharomyces) consume grape sugars and produce alcohol, CO₂, and heat. Red wines often ferment on skins to extract color and tannin; whites are usually pressed first. Temperature control and cap management are critical. On the ledger, fermentation is a good metaphor for transformation: inputs become outputs under rules you can log and audit.

Ledger: BARREL_AGING

Barrel aging

Many wines rest in oak barrels (or large neutral vessels) to soften tannins, develop aroma, and allow controlled oxygen pickup. Coopers, toast levels, and cellar climate all shape the outcome. Some wines skip wood entirely. Educationally, barrel aging highlights time-delayed quality and batch identity — ideal for discussing long-lived records versus ephemeral dashboards.

Ledger: BOTTLING

Bottling & closure

Finished wine is filtered or stabilized, dosed if needed, then filled under hygiene controls. Cork, screwcap, or other closures affect aging trajectory. Labels carry legal and marketing claims. This stage is where identity meets regulation — lot codes, TTB-style truth-in-labeling, and export paperwork are all traceability exercises.

Ledger: TRANSPORT

Transport & cold chain

Bottles move by truck, container, or air to distributors, export markets, or direct-to-consumer hubs. Temperature spikes are a common quality risk. Maps, routes, and handoffs are a strong fit for WineLedger’s geo enrichment ideas — distance, region codes, and corridor hints can sit beside each block’s metadata.

Ledger: RETAIL

Retail & the glass

Shops, restaurants, and subscription clubs are where story, price, and provenance meet the buyer. Staff training and consumer trust depend on consistent information. A tamper-evident ledger narrative — even a simplified one — can support discussions of fraud, recalls, and sustainability marketing claims.

A little historical context

Wine is among the oldest fermented beverages in the archaeological record, with production spread by trade, empire, and religion. Techniques now considered “modern” — temperature control, sterile filtration, scientific viticulture — sit atop centuries of craft knowledge. WineLedger is not a history simulator, but framing why transparency matters today (global supply chains, climate stress, consumer expectations) helps students see blockchains and ledgers as tools for stewardship, not only speculation.

Ideas to deepen the educational experience

Mix and match these with your course level (STEM outreach, hospitality, supply-chain, or digital humanities).

Map events to data fields

Have learners list what they would log at each stage (temperature, vessel ID, CO₂ venting, TTB lot, GPS). Compare with the JSON your API already returns.

Integrity & “why chain?”

Discuss hash links and immutability using the genesis block and tampering scenarios. Relate to food recalls and counterfeit bottles in the news.

Slow simulation + journaling

Use pace_ms so each visual lingers; students sketch or write a one-sentence hypothesis before the next event arrives.

Sustainability & ethics

Add a classroom debate: what should a ledger prove — carbon, water, fair labor? Which claims belong on chain vs. in a separate report?

Geo & routing literacy

Use metadata like region_code and route distance to introduce maps, great-circle vs. road routing, and limitations of synthetic data.

Creative coding bridge

Tie Hydra parameters (hue, motion) to scientific meaning — e.g., map heat stress to saturation — and critique whether the mapping is fair or misleading.
